SPEAKER NOTES — Entropy-Guided Token Importance for PPO
========================================================

SLIDE 1: The Problem
--------------------
So the standard PPO objective treats every token the same — there's a
uniform 1/T average over all positions. But intuitively, some tokens
matter way more than others. If you look at a math response like
"Let x = 3 + 4 = 7, therefore the answer is \boxed{7}", the "7" is
everything — that's where the reward comes from. The "Let" and
"therefore" are just boilerplate. So the question we're asking is:
can we weight tokens by how important they are, and does that help
PPO learn faster or with less KL drift?

There's recent work called TI-DPO that showed this works really well
for DPO. So we wanted to know: does it also work for PPO?


SLIDE 2: Prior Work — TI-DPO
-----------------------------
TI-DPO by Yang et al. introduced per-token importance weights into the
DPO loss. They tried several scoring methods — gradient norm of the
embedding, attention scores, a hybrid of gradient plus a Gaussian prior,
and TD error from a value function. These give you a weight w(t) per
token, and you multiply the DPO loss by it. They showed +2 to 5% gains
on preference benchmarks, so it clearly works for DPO.


SLIDE 3: Our First Step — Port to PPO
---------------------------------------
Our first idea was straightforward: take these exact same importance
scores from TI-DPO — the gradient norm, the attention weights, the
hybrid score — and plug them into the PPO clipped surrogate loss as
per-token weights.

But there's a fundamental difference between DPO and PPO that makes
this tricky. In DPO, you have paired sequences — a chosen and a rejected
response. The importance scores naturally capture "which tokens differ
between the good and bad response." In PPO, you just have a single
sequence and a scalar reward at the end. There's no rejected response
to compare against, so gradient attribution and attention scores don't
have a clear interpretation anymore.

On top of that, PPO runs for hundreds of gradient steps, while DPO is
typically a single pass. So any systematic error in the weights
compounds over time.


SLIDE 4: Phase 1 Results
-------------------------
And indeed, when we ran this, the paper's methods actually performed
worse than vanilla PPO. The hybrid method gets 8% less reward than
the baseline. It does have lower KL, so it's not completely useless,
but it's clearly not optimizing what we care about. The takeaway here
is that we need importance signals that are native to PPO, not borrowed
from DPO.


SLIDE 5: Entropy as Importance
-------------------------------
So here's our idea. Instead of gradient attribution or attention, we
use the model's own entropy at each token position. The entropy
H(π(·|s_t)) measures how uncertain the model is about what token comes
next at position t. We normalize it to have mean 1 and use it directly
as the weight in the PPO loss.

Why entropy? Because high entropy means the model sees multiple
plausible next tokens — it hasn't decided what to do yet. That's
exactly where a gradient update is most valuable. Low entropy means the
model is already confident — maybe it's a function word, maybe it
already knows the answer there. Either way, there's less to gain from
updating that position.

And crucially, this is free — we already have the logits from the
forward pass, we just compute the entropy from them. No extra forward
or backward pass needed.


SLIDE 6: Entropy Intuition
---------------------------
Let me make this concrete with an example. If the model is solving
"What is 15 times 7?", look at the entropy at each token position.
"Let" has entropy 0.2 — the model knows how to start, there's really
only one natural continuation. "me" is 0.1, even more obvious.

But when we get to the equals sign — entropy jumps to 1.4. The model
has to actually compute the answer here. There are many numbers it
could output. This is the critical decision point. And "105" — the
actual answer — also has high entropy because the model genuinely
isn't sure whether to say 105 or some other number.

So entropy naturally identifies the decision points — the tokens where
the model's choice determines whether the answer is right or wrong.
These are exactly the positions where PPO's gradient signal is most
useful.


SLIDE 7: Doesn't grad log pi already know this?
-------------------------------------------------
Now you might ask: doesn't the gradient itself already encode which
tokens matter? After all, ∇log π is the direction the model would move
to make each token more likely.

The key distinction is in this table. A confident correct token like
"the" has small gradient and low entropy — fine, we agree it doesn't
matter. But a confident WRONG token — say the model confidently outputs
"52" instead of "105" — also has small gradient AND low entropy. The
gradient won't fix this because the model is confident.

But an uncertain token — where the model could go either way between
the right and wrong answer — has high entropy. That's where a gradient
update has the most leverage. A small push in the right direction can
tip the balance. So entropy captures something orthogonal to what the
gradient magnitude tells you.


SLIDE 8: Entropy Works Then Doesn't
-------------------------------------
So we tried it. We applied entropy weights to the PPO loss and trained
for 150 episodes. At episode 100, entropy weighting is 10% ahead of
the baseline — great, it works!

But then by episode 150, the baseline has caught up and surpassed it.
What happened? The weights stay fixed throughout training, but the model
is changing underneath. The tokens that were uncertain at the start may
no longer be the important ones later. The fixed weights become stale,
and the small error they introduce on each step accumulates over hundreds
of gradient updates until it's large enough to hurt.


SLIDE 9: Why Fixed Weights Degrade
------------------------------------
Here's the math behind what we just saw. When you weight the gradient by
w(t), the estimator becomes biased — the bias equals the covariance
between the weights and the per-token gradient signal. If the weights
correlate with the gradient magnitude — which they do, that's the whole
point — then this covariance is non-zero.

Early in fine-tuning, the policy is still adapting, gradient variance is
high, and the variance reduction from entropy weighting outweighs the
bias. So you learn faster. But later, the policy stabilizes, variance
drops naturally, and now the accumulated bias from all those weighted
updates starts to dominate. The gradient is pointing in a slightly wrong
direction on every step, and eventually that adds up.

And to be clear, "early" and "late" here refers to the RL fine-tuning
process, not pretraining. Even though the base model is already
pretrained and capable, the PPO process itself has dynamics — the policy
changes most in the first few hundred updates, then stabilizes.


SLIDE 10: AITI
---------------
The fix is actually quite simple. Instead of keeping the entropy weights
at full intensity the whole time, we decay them. We introduce an
intensity parameter epsilon that starts at 1 and linearly decays to 0
over training. When epsilon is 1, you get full entropy weighting. When
epsilon is 0, you get standard uniform PPO.

Think of it like learning rate scheduling — just as you decrease the
step size over training, you decrease the weighting intensity. The
learning rate controls how far you step; AITI controls where the
gradient focuses. Both should start high and decay.


SLIDE 11: AITI Results
-----------------------
And this works. AITI-Entropy Pareto-dominates the PPO baseline — it
gets higher reward AND 2.5 times lower KL divergence. That means the
policy improves more while staying closer to the reference model.
Meanwhile, fixed entropy weighting without decay is dominated — it
degrades just as we predicted. The decay schedule is what makes entropy
weighting viable in PPO.


SLIDE 12: MOAI Motivation
---------------------------
AITI has one limitation — you have to choose the decay schedule
yourself. How many steps to decay over? Linear or quadratic? We wanted
to remove this hyperparameter entirely.

The idea is simple: at each step, choose epsilon to minimize the mean
squared error of the gradient estimator. MSE decomposes into bias
squared plus variance — this is just the standard bias-variance
decomposition. A gradient with lower MSE relative to the true gradient
gives better policy updates on average.

It turns out this has a closed-form solution, which we call MOAI. You
just compute two statistics from the training data — a covariance and a
variance term — estimate them with an exponential moving average, and
plug into the formula. The monotone constraint ensures epsilon only
decreases, preventing oscillation.


SLIDE 13: MOAI Finding
------------------------
Here's the interesting finding. MOAI doesn't decay epsilon to zero. It
converges to around 0.3 to 0.5 and stays there.

This might seem contradictory — didn't we just say you need to decay to
zero to eliminate bias? Well, AITI decays to zero as a conservative
choice — it eliminates ALL bias, which is safe but gives up all the
variance reduction too. MOAI finds a more nuanced answer: at epsilon
0.3, the remaining bias squared is negligible, but you're still getting
meaningful variance reduction from the entropy weights. Going from 0.3
to 0 removes almost no bias but gives up real benefit. So the optimal
strategy is permanent partial weighting.

And the results confirm this — MOAI gets the highest reward of any
method, while AITI gets the best KL efficiency. They represent different
points on the Pareto frontier, and both dominate vanilla PPO.


SLIDE 14: GSM8K Results
-------------------------
Moving to real math benchmarks. On GSM8K with a 1.5B model, we see that
all methods improve about 8% over the base model, but the differences
between entropy methods and baseline are within 1%. The reason is that
GSM8K is relatively easy for this model — the base already gets 55%, so
there's limited headroom for PPO, and even less room for entropy
weighting to differentiate.

These results are from the entropy-only rerun that's currently in
progress, so the numbers will be updated.


SLIDE 15: MATH-500 Results
----------------------------
MATH is much harder — competition-level problems. We're running this
with a 4B model and 500 episodes, and the early results at episode 40
show AITI-Entropy leading at 35.4% versus the baseline at 33.5%. This
is more promising because there's more room for improvement on this
harder task.

The final results with test accuracy are still running and will be
filled in once they complete.


SLIDE 16: Future Directions
-----------------------------
Looking ahead, the main thing we need is harder tasks where PPO makes a
bigger difference, so the entropy weighting has more to amplify. Code
generation is a natural fit — variable names and function signatures have
low entropy because they're determined by context, while the actual
logic has high entropy because there are many valid implementations.
We're already running MBPP experiments.

Scaling to larger models should also help — a 7B model has more capacity
to benefit from focused gradient updates.

On the methods side, applying entropy weighting to the KL penalty, not
just the policy loss, could be interesting. And integrating with GRPO,
which is gaining popularity as a PPO alternative, would broaden the
applicability.

The core insight to take away is that token importance weighting is
really gradient variance reduction. And like all variance reduction
techniques, it helps most when variance is the bottleneck — small
batches, sparse rewards, early in training, hard tasks.
